Checkride Strategy

How to Pass Your CFI Checkride on the First Attempt

BY CLEAR CUT CFI · JUN 11, 2026 · 6 MIN READ

The initial CFI checkride has a reputation, and it's earned. Year after year, it carries one of the lowest first-attempt pass rates of any FAA practical test — and the pilots failing it are not weak pilots. They're commercial certificate holders who flew a clean ride two months earlier. So what's going wrong?

The short answer: they prepared for the wrong test. The CFI practical doesn't ask whether you know the material. It asks whether you can teach it — and teaching is a separate skill that most candidates never deliberately practice. Here's the preparation plan that fixes that.

1. Understand what's actually being tested

Every other checkride you've taken evaluated you as a pilot. This one evaluates you as an instructor. The examiner is asking one question all day: "Would I send my own student to this person?"

That changes everything about how you should prepare. A perfect recitation of the four levels of learning, delivered like a memorized speech, doesn't answer that question. A slightly imperfect explanation, delivered clearly on a whiteboard with a real-world example and a check for understanding, does.

2. Build your entire prep around the ACS

The Flight Instructor for Airplane Category ACS (FAA-S-ACS-25, effective May 31, 2024) is the literal grading rubric for your checkride. The examiner is required to test to it. That makes it the single most underused study tool in CFI prep.

Print it. Read the front matter, not just the tasks — the introduction explains how the examiner builds your plan of action. Then go task by task and ask, for each knowledge element: could I teach this right now, with a marker in my hand? If the answer is no, that element goes on your study list. If the answer is yes, prove it with a teach-back.

This is the difference between studying everything (slow, exhausting, unfocused) and studying what you'll be graded on.

3. Treat the FOI as half the battle — because it is

The Fundamentals of Instruction make up Area I of the ACS, and they typically open the oral. Examiners use the FOI to set the tone: handle it fluently and the rest of the day gets easier; stumble through it and you've invited deeper digging for the next several hours.

The trap is that FOI material feels easy to read and is genuinely hard to apply. Most busts here aren't from forgetting a definition — they're from being unable to apply one. "Define the law of primacy" is a softball. "Your student learned a bad habit from a previous instructor — which law of learning explains why this is a problem, and what do you do about it?" is the real question.

Work through our free FOI Study Guide, then test yourself with the free FOI quizzes — one for each task, A through F, with randomized question banks so you can't memorize your way to false confidence.

4. Teach it. Out loud. On a whiteboard. Repeatedly.

This is the step most candidates skip, and it's the one that decides the outcome. Reading about teaching is not teaching, the same way reading about landings is not landing.

Pick a task — say, the region of reverse command — and teach it to an empty room, out loud, with drawings. It will be rough the first time. That roughness is exactly what you're eliminating before checkride day. Then teach it to a person: a fellow CFI candidate, your instructor, a patient friend. Teaching to a human forces you to handle questions, read confusion, and adjust — which is precisely what the examiner wants to see.

A good rule: for every hour you spend reading, spend at least thirty minutes teaching out loud.

5. Know the classic busts before you meet them

Patterns repeat across initial CFI checkrides. The most common trouble spots:

  • FOI application questions. Definitions delivered, scenarios fumbled. Fix it by practicing scenario-based FOI questions, not flashcards alone.
  • Endorsements and logbook entries. You will almost certainly be handed a scenario — a solo, a flight review, a checkride recommendation — and asked exactly which endorsements apply. Know the current revision of AC 61-65 and be able to navigate it quickly.
  • Stall/spin awareness. You did the spin training for your 61.183 endorsement — but can you teach spin aerodynamics, entry, and recovery on a whiteboard? Theory taught poorly here ends checkrides.
  • Teaching maneuvers while flying them. Talking and flying at the same time degrades both until you've practiced it. Narrate every maneuver on every prep flight until it feels normal.

6. Fly a mock checkride — the whole thing

A week or two out, have an experienced CFI run you through a realistic mock: a few hours of oral, then the flight, with no coaching until the debrief. The goal isn't a confidence boost — it's finding the weak spots while they're still free to find. Treat the debrief list as your final study plan.

7. Have a checkride-day plan

Decisions you make in advance don't drain you on the day. Organize your documents and endorsements the night before. Bring your lesson plans, your marked-up ACS, and your source documents — examiners expect an instructor to reference materials, not perform from memory. Eat. Arrive early. And when you don't know an answer, do what you'd want your own student to do: say so, then find it in the right document. That's not a weakness on this checkride — it's the job.

The bottom line

The CFI checkride rewards a specific kind of preparation: ACS-driven, FOI-heavy, and built around actually teaching out loud. Candidates who prepare that way walk in with an unfair advantage — because they're not hoping to pass a test. They're showing up already doing the job.
