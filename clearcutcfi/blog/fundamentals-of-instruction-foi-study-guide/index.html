Fundamentals of Instruction (FOI) Study Guide: All 6 Tasks

BY CLEAR CUT CFI · JUN 10, 2026 · 7 MIN READ

The Fundamentals of Instruction are Area I of the Flight Instructor ACS — six tasks, lettered A through F, that cover how people learn and how instructors teach. They usually open the CFI oral, which means they set the tone for your entire checkride. Handle them with confidence and the examiner relaxes. Fumble them and every later answer gets scrutinized harder.

This guide walks all six tasks in plain English: what each one covers, what examiners actually probe, and where candidates slip. The source document for everything here is the FAA's Aviation Instructor's Handbook (FAA-H-8083-9) — read it alongside this guide, then test yourself with the free quiz for each task.

Task A — Effects of Human Behavior and Communication on the Learning Process

Task A is about the human in the right seat. It covers basic human needs and motivation, defense mechanisms, stress and anxiety, and how communication actually works — and breaks — between instructor and student.

The classic exam angle is defense mechanisms: denial, repression, displacement, rationalization, compensation, projection, fantasy, and reaction formation. Memorizing the list is the easy part. The real question is recognition in context: "Your student blames every bad landing on the airplane. What's happening, and what do you do?" That's rationalization (or projection, depending on the framing) — and your answer needs to include how you'd respond as the instructor, not just the label.

Also be ready for communication barriers — confusion between the symbol and the symbolized object, overuse of abstractions, interference — and what an instructor does about each.

Task B — Learning Process

Task B is the densest task in the FOI, and the one examiners mine the hardest. The big frameworks:

  • Levels of learning: rote, understanding, application, correlation. Know them as a ladder, and know that flight training aims for correlation — the student connecting what they've learned to new situations you never explicitly taught.
  • Laws of learning: readiness, exercise, effect, primacy, intensity, recency. The exam favorite is primacy — what's learned first is learned best, which is why unteaching a bad habit is so much harder than teaching it right the first time.
  • Domains of learning: cognitive (knowing), affective (attitudes), psychomotor (doing) — and the idea that flight training works all three at once.
  • Memory and forgetting, learning plateaus, and transfer of learning (positive and negative).

Expect application questions: "Your student plateaued on landings — what's happening and how do you respond?" A good answer names the plateau as a normal part of the learning curve, keeps the student's morale intact, and adjusts the lesson rather than drilling harder.

Task C — Course Development, Lesson Plans, and Classroom Training Techniques

Task C is where teaching gets practical: training syllabi, blocks of learning, lesson plan structure, and teaching methods (lecture, guided discussion, demonstration-performance, e-learning and more). The demonstration-performance method matters most for flight instructors — explanation, demonstration, student performance, instructor supervision, and evaluation — because it's how you'll teach nearly every maneuver.

Lesson plans deserve their own deep dive — we wrote one: CFI Lesson Plans: What Actually Matters. The short version for Task C: know the parts of a lesson plan, why each part exists, and how a plan fits into a syllabus's blocks of learning.

Task D — Student Evaluation, Assessment, and Testing

Task D covers how instructors measure learning: traditional assessment (written quizzes and tests) versus authentic assessment (judging real performance against real standards), characteristics of effective assessment, oral quizzing technique, and critiques.

Know the traits of a good assessment — objective, flexible, comprehensive, constructive, organized, thoughtful, specific — and be ready to explain collaborative assessment, where the student self-assesses first and you guide the debrief. Examiners like this task because it predicts how you'll actually debrief students after flights.

Task E — Elements of Effective Teaching in a Professional Environment

Task E is about you: instructor professionalism, sincerity and acceptance of the student, proper personal appearance and habits, safety practices and accident prevention as a mindset, and the instructor's responsibilities — helping students learn, providing adequate instruction, setting standards, and evaluating honestly. It also covers minimizing student frustrations: motivate, keep students informed, approach them as individuals, give credit when due, criticize constructively.

This task sounds soft and gets tested concretely: "Your student wants to attempt their checkride and you don't think they're ready. What do you do?" Your answer demonstrates professional integrity — the heart of Task E.

Task F — Elements of Effective Teaching that Include Risk Management and Accident Prevention

Task F is the newest emphasis in the FOI: teaching students to manage risk, not just fly maneuvers. Know the risk management tools cold — PAVE (Pilot, Aircraft, enVironment, External pressures), IMSAFE for pilot fitness, the 5P check, and the DECIDE model for in-flight decisions — and, more importantly, how you'd build them into a student's training rather than recite them.

Be ready to discuss hazard versus risk, mitigation strategies, and scenario-based training: giving students realistic situations where the "right answer" is a judgment call, then debriefing the decision-making, not just the stick-and-rudder.

How to study the FOI (without losing your mind)

  1. Read one task's material at a time in the Aviation Instructor's Handbook — not the whole book in a weekend.
  2. Quiz immediately. Take the free quiz for that task. Wrong answers are your study list; the randomized banks keep retakes honest.
  3. Teach it back. Out loud, to a person or an empty room, with a whiteboard. If you can't explain it simply, you're at rote — and the checkride demands application and correlation.
  4. Return to weak tasks weekly. The law of recency works on you, too.
