CFI Lesson Plans: What Actually Matters (and What the FAA Says)

BY CLEAR CUT CFI · JUN 9, 2026 · 6 MIN READ

Few topics generate more anxiety in CFI prep than lesson plans. Candidates spend weeks hand-building enormous binders, hear a rumor that examiners hate store-bought plans, panic, rebuild, and arrive at the checkride exhausted by paperwork that was never the point.

Let's clear it up: what a lesson plan is for, what the FAA actually says about where your plans can come from, and how to build a set you can genuinely teach from.

First: what the FAA actually says about commercial lesson plans

This question has an official answer. The FAA's Aviation Instructor's Handbook (FAA-H-8083-9) addresses commercially developed lesson plans directly: it describes them as acceptable for most training situations — including use by flight instructor applicants on their practical tests — while cautioning that an instructor still needs to review and understand any plan they didn't write, and adapt it to the student and the situation.

So the binary question — "are pre-made plans legal on the checkride?" — is settled. The real question the examiner is evaluating is different: can you teach from the plan in front of you? A hand-written plan you can't deliver is worth less than a purchased plan you've internalized, marked up, and made your own. The plan is a tool. You are the instructor.

The anatomy of a lesson plan that works

The Aviation Instructor's Handbook lays out the standard parts of a lesson plan. Here's each one, with the job it does:

  • Objective. What the student will be able to do by the end — stated in terms of performance, not topics covered. "Student demonstrates steep turns within ACS standards" beats "cover steep turns."
  • Content / elements. The actual material: the aerodynamics, procedures, common errors, and standards for the subject. This is the meat — and the part you teach from.
  • Schedule. Rough time allocation, so a one-hour ground session doesn't spend forty minutes on the introduction.
  • Equipment. Whiteboard, model airplane, charts, the airplane itself — whatever the lesson requires.
  • Instructor's actions. What you'll do: explain, demonstrate, question, supervise.
  • Student's actions. What they'll do: listen, respond, perform, self-assess. If the student's column is empty, it's a lecture, not a lesson.
  • Completion standards. How both of you will know the objective was met — ideally tied straight to the ACS.

Two additions worth making to every maneuver plan: common errors (the examiner loves asking for them, and they're half of real instructing) and references (the handbook chapter, ACS task, and any relevant regs — so you can open the source on demand).

A plan is not a script

The single biggest lesson plan mistake on checkrides isn't the plan — it's the delivery. Candidates write paragraphs, then read them aloud. The examiner isn't grading your reading voice; they're checking whether an actual student would learn from you.

A working lesson plan is closer to an approach plate than an essay: structured prompts you glance at while your attention stays on the student and the whiteboard. If your plan can't be taught from at a glance, it isn't finished — it's a first draft of your knowledge, and the next step is condensing it into something teachable.

The test is simple: hand your plan to yourself cold, set a 15-minute timer, and teach the topic out loud using only the plan as a reference. Where you stall, the plan (or your understanding) needs work.

Organize the set around the ACS

The Flight Instructor ACS (FAA-S-ACS-25) is organized into areas of operation and tasks — and your examiner builds the checkride's plan of action from it. Organize your lesson plan binder the same way, in the same order, with a table of contents. On checkride day, when the examiner says "teach me Task X," you flip straight to it. That thirty seconds of crisp organization communicates more professionalism than an hour of talking about professionalism.

How the examiner actually uses your plans

Expect some combination of: asking how you built or chose your plans, picking a task and having you teach it, and probing the parts — "what's your completion standard here, and where does it come from?" Each of these is really a question about whether the plans are yours in the way that matters: understood, adapted, and deliverable.

Which is why the preparation that wins isn't more formatting. It's reps: teach from your plans, out loud, until the plan fades into the background and the teaching is what's left. That's also the heart of FOI Task C — covered in our FOI Study Guide, and tested in the free Task C quiz.

The bottom line

Lesson plans are checkride infrastructure, not the checkride itself. Build (or adapt) a set with the standard parts, organize it around the ACS, strip it down until you can teach from it at a glance — and then spend your remaining prep time teaching, because that's what's actually being graded.
