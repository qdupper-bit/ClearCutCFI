Oral Exam

The CFI Oral Exam: What DPEs Actually Ask

BY CLEAR CUT CFI · JUN 8, 2026 · 6 MIN READ

The CFI oral is long — typically the longest oral exam in your flying career — and that length scares candidates into the wrong kind of preparation: trying to memorize everything. But the oral isn't random. It follows the ACS, it leans on predictable question patterns, and it's graded on something most candidates never practice: answering like an instructor.

Here's how the day is structured, the question types that do the damage, and the answer framework that changes how examiners hear you.

How the oral is structured

The examiner builds a plan of action from the Flight Instructor ACS (FAA-S-ACS-25). In practice, most CFI orals open with the Fundamentals of Instruction — Area I, Tasks A through F — before moving into the technical subject areas: regulations, airworthiness, aerodynamics, performance, weather, airspace, endorsements, and the rest.

That ordering matters strategically. The FOI portion is where the examiner forms their first impression of you as a teacher. Walk in fluent there — our FOI Study Guide and the free task quizzes exist for exactly this — and the technical portion tends to stay conversational. Struggle early, and expect deeper probing all day.

The golden rule: answer like a teacher, not a student

On every previous checkride, a correct, complete answer was the goal. On this one, the examiner is role-playing your future student as much as testing you. The framework that works:

  1. Explain it simply. Lead with the concept in plain English, as if to a 20-hour student — not with the most technical version you know.
  2. Give an example or draw it. Reach for the whiteboard without being asked. A thirty-second sketch of left-turning tendencies says "instructor" louder than five minutes of talking.
  3. Check for understanding. Close the loop the way you would with a student: "Does that track?" or "Want me to take that one layer deeper?"

Explain → example → check. It feels unnatural the first ten times. By the twentieth rep it's just how you talk — and it's the single highest-leverage habit you can build before the oral.

The question patterns that bust candidates

1. Endorsement scenarios

The most predictable trap in the CFI oral. You'll get a scenario — "Your student is ready for their first solo. What do you need to do?" — and the examiner expects you to walk the requirements: the training, the knowledge test you administer, and the specific endorsements, using the current revision of AC 61-65 as your reference. Variations include solo cross-country, flight reviews, additional ratings, and the recommendation endorsements you'll sign for your own students' checkrides.

You don't need every endorsement memorized. You need to know the categories, find the right one fast, and explain what it certifies. Fumbling here reads as "not ready to supervise students," which is why it's such a frequent bust.

2. FOI application questions

Not "define the levels of learning" but "your student keeps reverting to an old habit under pressure — what's going on?" (Primacy, and a plan to retrain.) The fix is practicing FOI material as scenarios, not flashcards.

3. Teach-me questions

"Teach me adverse yaw like I'm a student pilot." This is where the marker comes out and your lesson plans earn their keep. The examiner is watching for organization, accuracy, and whether you check on your "student" — not for a flawless lecture.

4. What-would-you-do scenarios

"Your student shows up for a lesson and mentions they didn't sleep last night." "A renter pressures you to sign off a flight review you're not comfortable with." These test judgment and professionalism — FOI Tasks E and F in disguise. Strong answers name the risk tools (IMSAFE, PAVE) naturally and land on a decision you can defend.

5. The depth probe

Examiners follow correct answers with "why?" until they find your floor. That's not hostility — it's calibration. Which leads to the most important skill of the day:

Know where to find it — and never bluff

Instructors are not encyclopedias; they're people who can locate and interpret the right reference quickly. Bring your source documents — FAR/AIM, the ACS, the Aviation Instructor's Handbook, the PHAK, AC 61-65 — tabbed and familiar. When you hit your limit, the professional answer is: "I don't want to guess on that — let me show you where it lives," followed by actually finding it.

A bluffed answer, discovered, poisons everything after it. A confident lookup demonstrates exactly the behavior you'll be modeling for students. One of these ends checkrides; the other passes them.

How to prepare

  • Study by ACS task, asking "how would I teach this?" for every knowledge element.
  • Drill the FOI to fluency with the free quizzes — randomized banks, one per task.
  • Do mock orals out loud. Trade sessions with another candidate or your CFI; rehearse the explain → example → check pattern until it's automatic.
  • Rehearse the endorsement scenarios until navigating AC 61-65 takes seconds, not minutes.
